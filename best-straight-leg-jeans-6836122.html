Finding the right pair of jeans is such a nuanced journey, isn't it? Slip on a pair in a fitting room and you're guaranteed to have a slew of questions run through your mind that'll influence your purchasing decision. There's the length to consider (Will I need to get these tailored?) to the wash (Do I *really* need another pair of dark wash denim?" — and of course, the fit (Does this flatter my body shape?). 

One such style of jeans that has withstood the test of time is the straight leg jean. How to Pick the Right Straight-Leg Jeans

Length

Traditionally, straight-leg jeans hit just above the ankle, says Duer founder Gary Lenett. "That said, we are seeing some straight-leg fits with longer inseams that hit the shoe for a slightly stacked look." Think the Mother Denim The Maven Heel Jeans and Khaite Straight Leg Jeans, which have carefully-placed waistlines and hem lengths to stretch your stems for miles. But ultimately, the length of your jeans will depend on a few things — your personal style and functionality being the main factors.

You should also consider the best length for your choice of footwear for ease of styling. "If you’re looking for everyday jeans, choose a length that will best suit an everyday sneaker," says Lenett. Aside from footwear, he recommends taking the season into consideration. "If you’re living in a wet climate, for instance, opt for a shorter length to ensure your jeans last for multiple seasons," he says.

Material

Jeans are made out of cotton fibers, and many of today's styles are made with materials like lycra or spandex for some added stretch, notes Lenett. "A lot of people still associate stretch with loss of shape retention or lack of structure, but there’s been a lot of innovation in denim over the last decade — it’s now possible to have the look of ‘90s rigid denim while also having just enough stretch integrated for comfort," says Lenett. "For that person who seeks to avoid stretch altogether, rigid raw denims are the best alternative."

There's also sustainability to consider. Lenett notes that there’s a big focus on integrating recycled fibers in place of virgin synthetics. To take things a step further, some brands are integrating "performance fibers like COOLMAX® with antimicrobial and cooling properties so jeans can be worn day after day without washing," says Lenett.

Rise

Choosing the right rise for your denim is all about your personal style and body type. Lenett explains that low-rise jeans, like the Gap Low Rise Stride, hit on the hips and sit below your natural waist while mid-rise jeans, like the Pistola Bobbies, sit midway between your hips and belly button. "Mid-rise jeans offer more coverage than a low-rise but can feel less constricting than a high-rise, which typically sits at or above the belly button, accentuating the waist and providing more of an hourglass silhouette," he says. 

There's also ultra-high-rise denim to consider, which sits even higher than high-rise and closer to the lower ribs. Take the Paige Ultra High Rise jeans and their leg-lengthening silhouette, which sits a bit above the waistline for the ultimate elongated look. While each style works differently for various body types, Lenett says that high-rise jeans offer added support around the hips and stomach, making it a solid option if you’re looking for pants with a snug fit across the midsection.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • ​​What women's jeans are in style for 2023?

    Straight-leg jeans are a huge hit for 2023, as it's a shift away from skinny jeans and a throwback to ’90s trends, says Lenett. "It's the perfect blend between a classic, relaxed style that offers a vintage look but at the same time, a modern, flattering cut."

    Besides straight-leg jeans, there are several women's jeans that are in style this year. For one, carpenter jeans are making their waves among fashion lovers, as they're a nod to the comfort of cargo pants we all know and love. "Many people [are] looking for a twist on their essential styles that are still functional, making this the perfect entryway for the cargo pant," says Sethi. "Look for a pair in a darker denim or olive color for maximum versatility."

    A good pair of relaxed-fit jeans with a looser silhouette is also sought after this year. As Sethi notes, since the relaxed style can read casual, try pairing this type of denim with a more structured top to dress it up a bit (think: blazers, blouses, and sweaters).

  • What shoes should I wear with straight-leg jeans?

    Straight leg jeans are so versatile and can be worn for both casual outings and dressier occasions alike. "We’re seeing a lot of people pair a longer straight leg with a dressy boot for a more sophisticated look," says Lenett. "In terms of what to avoid, calf or knee-high boots that you’re hoping to show off won’t complement the straight-leg fit." If you prefer to go more casual, you can never go wrong pairing your straight leg jeans with a pair of everyday sneakers.

  • What are the most comfortable straight-leg jeans?

    The most comfortable straight leg jeans are the ones that fit you the best. They should "be fitting throughout the leg, bottom, and waist," notes Sethi, and they should also pass the "sit test," which involves sitting down in your jeans and seeing if they dig into your stomach. If they do, you should go one size up. Also, pay attention to the type of closure the jeans have — while most will have a button and fly, some will have a button placket (a row of four or more buttons) or even a drawcord and elastic band, which Sethi says can be comfortable for those in the midst of a body transition. Some even come with a lace-up waistband (in back, no less), like the River Island Petite ‘90s Straight Jean.

    Also, consider the material of the denim and if it'll have a break-in period. "Raw or non-stretch denim, typically made from 100 percent cotton, rely on starches that result in a stiff and rigid feel," says Lenett. "The 'break-in period' is critical for wearability and comfort." In any case, you’ll know you have the right fit if your straight-leg jean is snug (not tight) throughout the thigh and loose from the knee down, says Lenett.
